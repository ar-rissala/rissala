You now know the **28 Arabic letters** and their **4 contextual forms**. But a crucial element is missing: how do you pronounce these letters? How do you know if <span class="arabic-inline">ب</span> is pronounced "ba," "bi," or "bu"?

The answer: **short vowels**, called **harakāt** (حَرَكَات) in Arabic. And this is where Arabic reveals its most fascinating logic.

## The fundamental concept: short vowels are NOT letters

This is **the most important point** in this article. In French, vowels (a, e, i, o, u) are full letters written in words. In Arabic, short vowels are **diacritical marks** — small symbols added above or below consonants.

They are not part of the word's "skeleton." They are like annotations that can be added or removed without changing the basic writing.

:::info
### 💡 Rissala analogy
Think of consonants as the **skeleton** of a word, and short vowels as the **flesh** around it. The skeleton is always written. The flesh is often implicit — the experienced reader reconstructs it mentally.
:::

## The 3 short vowels + sukun

Arabic has only **3 short vowels**. That is remarkably simple compared to French (which has about fifteen vowel sounds). Plus a special sign, sukun, indicating the absence of a vowel.

## Fatha: the "a" sound

**Fatha** (فَتْحَة) is a small slanted stroke placed **above** the consonant. It gives it the short "a" sound.

Fatha is the most frequent vowel in Arabic. It corresponds to the short, open "a" sound. The letter alone (<span class="arabic-inline">ب</span>) is silent. With fatha (<span class="arabic-inline">بَ</span>), it becomes "ba."

**Kasra** (كَسْرَة) is a small slanted stroke placed **below** the consonant. It gives it the short "i" sound.

## Damma: the "u" (oo) sound

**Damma** (ضَمَّة) is a miniature "waw" (<span class="arabic-inline">و</span>) placed **above** the consonant. It gives it the short "oo" sound (transcribed "u").

## Sukun: absence of vowel

**Sukun** (سُكُون) is a small circle placed above the consonant. It indicates the consonant is not followed by any vowel — it is "silent."

Par exemple, dans le mot <span class="arabic-inline">بَيْت</span> (bayt, « house »), le <span class="arabic-inline">يْ</span> door un sukun : le yā' ne se prononce pas « ya » ni « yi » ni « yu », mais simplement « y » (comme dans « bayt »).

:::info
### 📌 Visual summary
**Fatha** (ـَ) = stroke above = "a" | **Kasra** (ـِ) = stroke below = "i" | **Damma** (ـُ) = loop above = "u/oo" | **Sukun** (ـْ) = circle above = silence
:::

## Why short vowels are often absent

In everyday life — newspapers, books, websites, signs — short vowels **are not written**. Arabic is written mainly with the consonantal skeleton, and the reader reconstructs vowels through:

*   The **context** of the sentence
*   Their **knowledge of vocabulary**
*   The language's **morphological patterns**

Short vowels are written in only 3 contexts:

1.  **The Quran** — to ensure perfect recitation
2.  **Beginner textbooks** — to facilitate learning
3.  **Ambiguous texts** — when context is not enough

## Consonants without vowels = the skeleton of the word

Pour bien comprendre, regardons le mot « kataba » (he wrote) sous trois formes :

  <div class="step-card text-center">

  <div class="step-card text-center">
    <div class="text-xs text-muted-foreground mb-2">Other vocalization</div>
    <div class="arabic text-3xl mb-1">كُتُب</div>

Notice: the same consonants <span class="arabic-inline">ك ت ب</span> (k-t-b) produisent des mots différents selon les voyelles : **kataba** (he wrote) vs **kutub** (books). Le squelette consonantique est le même — ce sont les voyelles qui changent le sens.

:::warning
### ⚠️ Critical point
That is why short vowels are so important for beginners. Without them, the word <span class="arabic-inline">كتب</span> could be read kataba, kutub, kutib, kātib... Context (and later, your vocabulary) will guide you.
:::

Tanwīn is a grammatical concept (related to declension). Do not dwell on it now — simply remember it exists and is a "doubling + n."

## Summary

1.  Short vowels (**harakāt**) are **diacritics**, not letters.
2.  **Fatha** (ـَ) = a, **Kasra** (ـِ) = i, **Damma** (ـُ) = u, **Sukun** (ـْ) = silence.
3.  In everyday texts, short vowels are **absent**.
4.  Same consonants + different vowels = **different words** (k-t-b → kataba, kutub...).
5.  The Quran and beginner textbooks include vowels to facilitate reading.

The next article covers **long vowels** — full letters that, unlike harakāt, are an integral part of the word skeleton.
