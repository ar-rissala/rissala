In the previous article, you learned that short vowels (**harakāt**) are diacritics — small signs above or below consonants. They are often absent in everyday texts.

**Long vowels**, on the other hand, are fundamentally different. They are **real letters**, written in the word, part of the consonantal skeleton. They never disappear. They are always written.

## The fundamental distinction: diacritic vs letter

This is the most important concept to master at this stage of your learning. Let us summarize:

:::info
### 💡 Key concept
Short vowels are like **accents** that can be omitted. Long vowels are like **letters** that can never be removed. Without them, the word no longer exists.
:::

### Alif (ا) — long "ā"

**Alif** is the first letter of the Arabic alphabet. When it acts as a long vowel, it prolongs the "a" sound. It is preceded by a consonant carrying fatha.

Mechanism: **consonant + fatha + alif** = prolonged "ā" sound.

### Wāw (و) — long "ū"

Le **Wāw** comme voyelle longue prolonge le son « ou ». La consonne précédente door une damma.

Mechanism: **consonant + damma + wāw** = prolonged "ū" sound.

## Short vowel vs long vowel: the difference changes meaning

It is not just a matter of duration. In Arabic, vowel length **changes the meaning of the word**. Confusing a short and long vowel can create a total misunderstanding.

Look: the same consonants <span class="arabic-inline">ك ت ب</span> (k-t-b, root of "write") produce 4 completely different words depending on vowels (short and long). This is the **magic of the Arabic root system**.

:::warning
### ⚠️ Common mistake
NEVER confuse "ba" (بَ, fatha alone) with "bā" (بَا, fatha + alif). The first is a short sound, the second is held. This distinction is **semantically critical** in Arabic.
:::

## The mechanism: short vowel + long letter

Long vowels do not work alone. They always form a **pair** with the corresponding short vowel on the consonant that precedes them:

<div class="table-wrapper">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr><th>Short vowel</th><th>+ Long letter</th><th>= Long sound</th></tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td>Fatha (ـَ = a)</td>
        <td><span class="arabic-inline">ا</span> (Alif)</td>
        <td>ā (prolonged a)</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Damma (ـُ = u)</td>
        <td><span class="arabic-inline">و</span> (Wāw)</td>
        <td>ū (prolonged oo)</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Kasra (ـِ = i)</td>
        <td><span class="arabic-inline">ي</span> (Yā')</td>
        <td>ī (prolonged i)</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

It is a perfectly logical system. The short vowel "announces" the long vowel that follows. Fatha announces Alif, Damma announces Wāw, Kasra announces Yā'.

## Wāw and Yā': dual role (consonant or vowel)

Note: <span class="arabic-inline">و</span> (Wāw) et <span class="arabic-inline">ي</span> (Yā') are letters with a **dual function**. They can be:

*   **Consonnes** : quand elles doornt elles-mêmes une voyelle courte ou un sukun en début de syllabe
*   **Long vowels**: when they prolong the sound of the preceding consonant

Example with Wāw as consonant: <span class="arabic-inline">وَلَد</span> (walad = enfant/garçon). Ici, le wāw door une fatha — il se prononce « wa », c'est une consonne.

Example with Wāw as long vowel: <span class="arabic-inline">نُور</span> (nūr = light). Ici, le wāw prolonge le « u » de la consonne nūn — c'est une voyelle longue.

## Summary

1.  Long vowels are **real letters** : <span class="arabic-inline">ا</span> (ā), <span class="arabic-inline">و</span> (ū), <span class="arabic-inline">ي</span> (ī).
2.  They are **always written**, unlike short vowels.
3.  Each long vowel is **preceded by its corresponding short vowel**.
4.  Vowel length **changes the meaning** of the word (kataba ≠ kātaba).
5.  Wāw and Yā' have a **dual role**: consonant or long vowel depending on context.
